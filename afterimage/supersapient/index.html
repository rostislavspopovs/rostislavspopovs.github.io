Supersapient (2020-)

A foreword

Supersapient was and still is an incredibly personal project for me, and it has proved really difficult to document it retrospectively. What at the time was a small game that I started with a few friends in the summer between our A-Levels and University had grown into an amorphous entity of massive proportions. I see it as a labour of love for the art of game development and all the media and styles that influenced it, and it holds so much meaning for me personally that it was ever intended to.

I haven’t played the original demo in many years, but when I started writing about it I realised I needed to do just that. I did not expect it to be an emotional experience. I knew it would bring a wave of nostalgia, but I was left with my eyes red by the end of it. I am filled with so much pride for the work that I put into it, and deep shame for ever loosening my grip on it. This project represents my ADHD-driven tendencies all too well, and when I look at it, it stares right back at me as a reminder of the muddy waters that have plagued my work since I can remember. I wish that I pushed through and continued the project throughout my time at university. I remember that I did try, but all of the change and novelty of my new environment quickly overshadowed it. I was also a novice, and by that point the project collected a lot of technical debt through my mistakes. By the end of the demo, it became really difficult to continue development due to my management of the code, assets, and decisions that I made early on.

By 2024, I have learned a lot about game development. I have worked in the field professionally, and have assisted on countless projects since. I resumed the development, with the decision to begin anew. By early 2025, I had completely rewritten the movement system - making it feel much more robust and akin to games of the same genre - and began to prototype new mechanics which the player would unlock throughout their journey. By that time, I have learned the importance of planning, quick prototyping, and the laying of foundation before content. To admit that this iteration was faced with the same challenge of diminishing motivation hurts to admit, but unlike the original, I have left it in a manageable state, and I am still determined to honour this world that I have created and iterated on.

Original Basis

Supersapient is a 3D platformer that explores a protagonist’s journey through the afterlife. Depending on your interpretation of the prefix “super”, the name can mean ‘above human’, or ‘incredibly human’. The story is universal, abstract and spiritually agnostic, never mentioning a specific faith in-name and using as little human language as possible to capture the feeling of complete mystery. You are not completely alone in your journey, as you are very quickly introduced to the guide of this world - a small, geometric and ever so eccentric entity. As you embody your avatar, learning about the world, its systems and language, you get the impression that this world learns about you, too.

The game and story would take place over a series of short chapters, each one aesthetically distinct, ntroducing new mechanics with harder platforming challenges. At the end of each chapter, your skills are tested as you manoeuvre and outsmart geometric guardians. They are not your enemies; you don’t shoot them with guns. Their motivations seem to be outside of our concepts of morality and while you are not sure what they are testing you on, you appear to pass.

The final guardian is not like the rest. Its size colossal, and form ever-changing, it represents the final test. Your spiritual guide, the entity which you were sure was your companion, appears to communicate with the guardian, providing information of your journey. The guardian makes its final judgement in its court.

The World

The plane which you explore is otherworldly. It is nonsensical and geometric, but equally carved for you to explore. I took inspiration from abstract art and though it is not an encouraged practice in game development, it was developed in a fashion akin to lyrical abstraction in which each environment was sculpted on-the-go, considering what each environment would represent throughout the protagonist’s journey.

The 5 chapters are linked by a common design language - through colour and formation of unique crystal structures. Chapter 0 (which you play through in the demo) acts as an introduction and is deliberately desolate and a blank state. It is well-lit, with beams of light which turn into iridescent displays of colour. Chapters 1-4 each have a distinct colour and remain unique by borrowing examples of different crystal formations that you may find in the real world.

A conscious world

The Four Axis

As alluded to in the description, the game would capture a behavioural profile of the player, consisting of the 4 below axis:

  • Direct/Explorative
  • Calm/Neurotic
  • Empath/Apathetic
  • Consistency/Indecision

The colour choices for each zone are intentional, each being a test: At a later point I decided to split the introductory chapter into two - becoming chapter 0 and chapter 1 respectively. Chapter 0 was used to gather early information which could be used to weigh the model. Some of these metrics included:

  • How long you spend exploring the first room, and whether you actively look for secrets.
  • If you have any affinity to a specific sculpture presented to you
  • When presented with two paths, do you check both or choose one blindly?
  • A sound of a breaking twig plays as you walk down a long corridor; do you turn around?
  • Given the option to use Pyra as a slingshot, do you take this knowing it temporarily incapacitates them?

Non-Euclidean Geometry

A short level would be presented in between each chapter, which would showcase excerpts of the protagonist’s previous lives. Though non-Euclidean elements appear throughout the whole game, they are heavily concentrated within these segments. The gameplay shifts from platforming to exploration entirely during these sections to reflect this, with the levels existing outside of the main chapters.

Inspiring my thesis

The idea for my Bachelor’s thesis was inspired by my work on Supersapient. I wanted to explore the idea of player profiling further, and whether it was possible to use the gathered information as a driver for potential impact on the player’s emotional intelligence. After all, I wanted the players of Supersapient to leave the game having learnt something about themselves.

Further Development (2024)

Research

To prepare for redevelopment, I began to conduct research into different forms of spirituality and depictions of the afterlife. Although this would never be explicitly stated in-game, I decided to incorporate Bardo - the Buddhist state of transition between death and rebirth. I identify as agnostic, but have in recent years been drawn to Buddhism for its teachings, which are framed in the person’s real-life actions and thoughts. It does not demand eternal faith in a being above you, but instead exists as a school of thought aimed at achieving personal enlightenment. It focuses on the person’s ability to self-direct and self-assess their thoughts and actions. And though many believe the parables of the Buddha and Bardo to be real accounts, one can see them as entirely metaphorical and still fully practice Buddhism. I thought this would be a good fit for the game, which aimed to present the afterlife as an abstract journey, not committing to specific deities.

I began reading the Bardo Thodol - or rather ‘The Tibetan Book of the Dead’, a translated version adapted for Western audience by Walter Evans-Wentz. The text is intended to guide one through the experiences that the consciousness has after death, in the interval between death and the next rebirth.

There were a few striking similarities between the Bardo Thodol and my original plans for Supersapient:

  • The Bardo is said to consist of three stages: Chikhai Bardo (the moment of death); Chonyd Bardo (the experience of reality); and Sidpa Bardo (the rebirth). While not a 1-1 match, the bardo thodol depicts a journey of the soul through a set of distinct stages.
  • The Chonyd Bordo features “psychic residua of previous existences”, similar to the transitional levels in between the chapters of Supersapient.
  • Gandharva is the transitional entity which represents the consciousness, which the player could stand as a representation for.
  • The 5 Pure Lights - Each one has an associated colour and represents the state of mind
    • Blue - Purity and Healing
    • White - Learning and Knowledge
    • Red - Life Force and Preservation
    • Green - Balance and Harmony
    • Yellow - Equanimity

Mechanics

A lot of work was done in the new version. The game being a 3D platformer, it was crucial to make sure that the controls felt smooth and controllable. With the addition of new abilities, the player is able to slide and jump - both of which were missing from the original.
