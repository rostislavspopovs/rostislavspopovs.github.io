Synesthesia and Lyric Videos

A strange word

Synesthesia is a phenomenon which links two sensory experiences together, such as hearing and vision, or vision and touch. A recognisable example is associating certain letters and numbers with particular colours.

I have a mixed art/science background, and I find synesthesia to be a deeply fascinating subject. Nothing peaks my curiosity more than the inner workings of the brain, especially when it happens to project imagery, sounds, or other qualia by its own will.

Synesthesia in Art and Media

Wassily Kandinsky was a famous synesthete who brought his personal interpretations of music into his art. Much of his work was focused on the connection between the auditory and visual. In his book ‘Concerning the Spiritual In Art’ (1912), he described his associations. To him, yellow invoked feelings of grandeur like a trumpet, while blue invoked deep vibration like a cello. From left to right: Small Worlds IV (1922), Drawing for Point and Line to Plane (1925), Composition VII (1913)

The above is only a very narrow selection of his works, but through them Wassily distinctly shows how a static graphic can portray motion, rhythm and sequence through shape alone. Composition VII was one of Kandinsky’s self-proclaimed greatest works, reflecting his view of the political climate during the beginning of WWI. It shows us that synesthesia, while often described as a simple connection of real senses, can be expanded to one’s experiences, memories, and emotions. The reason that I am mentioning Composition VII is that it was foundational in the Lyrical Abstractionism movement, focusing on free, emotive strokes and musical composition.

Music and visuals…

Are, for me, inherently linked. I remember as far back as secondary school I would imagine my own music videos - specifically lyric videos - of whatever song I was listening to at the time. Imagining how each repeating progression or chorus could be visualised to enhance the meanings of the words, with elements transforming in-sync to the beats. These compositions seemed to generate spontaneously and would be persistent, with the same arrangements coming back to me even if I didn’t listen to the song after a few months.

Synesthesia is experienced in different ways for different people. While I cant speak for others, the imagery that I experience is not inherently visual or detailed, but are more akin to how most people would see an object when prompted to imagine it. I can identify certain shapes and colours, but most prominently - motion - when listening to music.

A vast majority of lyric videos tend to have a static background (or maybe some sort of visualiser which only had one setting) with the lyrics simply fading in and out. I always felt like more could be done in this space. This prompted me to try and recreate some of my ideas in After Effects:

Alice Merton - Vertigo

A lot of the animation was designed and created deliberately to try and recreate the imagery I felt from certain components of the music. In particular:

  • The low-frequency buzz at the start of the song -> faint static in the background.
  • The bass of the song -> wide but quick bursts of movement coming in from the sides, represented in the video by the fire graphics in the background.
  • The 5 successive beats at the end -> in my head this formed a line of some sort, and I represented this with slamming doors which mirrored the percussion. I am not sure why I gravitate towards lyric videos rather than live action, but if I were to guess, it is linked to my affinity with abstraction. I find abstract visuals and animations much easier to visualise and to imbue a certain concept/feeling into them.

The style is abstract, and was inspired by the works of Saul Bass such as his poster for the movie Vertigo (which I promise was a coincidence!) as well as the album art of the band Weathers. I unfortunately was not able to find the name of the artist.

Ruben - Running
  • Introductory Synths -> Dollop pattern (similar to a drop of ink in water) which is animated to match the pitch of the synth.
  • The general percussion to me felt muted which I reflected in a hard rectangular mask that moves towards the centre of the screen. The “click” sound on the hand is very sharp and periodic, which I manifested as the ray of a lighthouse. Bloom was added to incorporate the small echo that is present on each click.
